# DTS Approving Official (AO) as Certifying Officer

In the Defense Travel System (DTS), the Approving Official (AO) is the official who applies the APPROVED stamp to a travel authorization or voucher. When that approval results in payment, the AO is acting as a Certifying Officer (CO) and is personally exposed to pecuniary liability for erroneous payments. The role also requires a written DD Form 577 appointment and completion of Certifying Officer Legislation (COL) training before starting and annually thereafter. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

AOs are expected to review each voucher using a standardized checklist or comparable process. Public DTMO guidance says the AO should determine whether travel is necessary and appropriate to the mission, ensure claimed expenses are valid, verify receipts and supporting records, review pre-audit flags and reason codes, and return the document for correction if an expense is not within mission or the request for payment is inappropriate. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The public guidance also shows that the AO workload is broader than a single receipt check. AO review commonly includes trip dates and locations, travel mode, expenses, receipts, compliance indicators, duplicate expense checks, and special handling for vouchers created by a Non-DTS Entry Agent (NDEA). Navy and Marine Corps materials reinforce that AOs must verify mission necessity, JTR compliance, required receipts, and justification quality. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

This role is difficult because it is an additional duty for many senior NCOs and officers rather than a dedicated finance billet. The source material describes the AO as operating under separation-of-duties rules, unable to self-approve their own travel, and responsible for reviewing other people’s documents under time pressure. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The risk is not theoretical: public oversight reports repeatedly tie improper travel payments to inadequate AO review. A 2025 DoD OIG audit reported improper-and-unknown-payment rates of 9.73% for DoD Travel Pay DAF–DTS and 8.59% for DoD Travel Pay Army–DTS in FY 2024, while earlier oversight found that approving officials’ reviews were not adequate to prevent improper payments. GAO also reported that some vouchers were approved without required receipts because approving officials could not view the receipts attached in DTS. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The most common failure mode is missing or invalid receipts. DTMO reported that more than 70% of improper payments were due to inadequate or invalid receipts, and it listed examples such as lodging receipts without proof of payment, rental car receipts without required details, and airfare documentation that did not qualify as a valid receipt. Other recurring issues include duplicate expenses, mismatches between claimed amounts and receipts, and weak or missing justifications. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

Because of these recurring failures, some services and units have mandated structured AO checklists and stronger return practices. Public Navy, Marine Corps, and National Guard guidance emphasizes that vague justification text is not enough, that pre-audit flags must be explained in writing, and that documents should be returned for correction when supporting evidence is missing or inadequate. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The source material positions this role as a good fit for [[AI-assisted spec with human review]] and [[Controlled Reference Retrieval]] rather than autonomous decision-making. A useful assistant in this space would help an AO triage the queue, surface missing or risky evidence, and point to relevant policy references, while leaving approval, certification, denial, return, and all liability-bearing decisions to the human AO/CO. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]
