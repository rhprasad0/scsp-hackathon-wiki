# AO voucher review checklist and cognitive workload

AO voucher review checklist and cognitive workload refers to the structured set of checks that Defense Travel System approving officials (AOs) must perform on travel vouchers, and the operational burden created by that review. Public DTMO guidance describes AOs as responsible for determining whether travel is necessary and appropriate, verifying expenses and receipts, checking trip dates, locations, pre-audit flags, reason codes, advisories, and returning documents for traveler correction when items are not compliant. The same guidance explicitly recommends a standardized process or checklist to reduce mistakes and oversights. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The checklist burden is substantial because AOs are not merely clerical reviewers: they are the human approver who carries personal pecuniary liability when approval results in payment. DTMO and DoD guidance tie AO review to Certifying Officer responsibilities, including required training and appointment documentation. In this context, the review task is an additional duty for many personnel rather than a dedicated finance role, which helps explain why the checklist is both operationally important and cognitively demanding. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

Public checklist materials show that AO review spans multiple screens and evidence types. Navy guidance, for example, requires explicit affirmations across voucher, expenses, per diem, accounting, financial summary, other authorizations and pre-audits, and sign-and-submit screens, while also checking duplicate expenses, lodging and air receipts, rental car details, and justification quality. Service and local policies also warn that vague entries such as “auth,” “approved,” or a single period are not sufficient justification for pre-audit flags. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The cognitive workload is visible in the error patterns AOs are expected to catch. Public DTMO material says that more than 70% of improper payments are due to inadequate or invalid receipts, and lists common invalid receipt patterns such as pre-checkout folios, unitemized totals, screenshots, and reservation confirmations instead of actual receipts. Other recurring failure modes include duplicate vouchers, lodging expenses paid twice, incorrect mileage, and amounts that do not match receipts. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

Oversight reports indicate that these problems are persistent and system-wide. DoD OIG and GAO reporting show continuing improper-payment rates in DTS, with documented shortcomings in the existing Travel Policy Compliance Tool, including inability to identify missing documentation or receipt-to-amount mismatches. This means AOs remain responsible for checking failure modes that the automated compliance layer does not reliably catch. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The public record also shows that commands have responded by formalizing checklists and tightening controls. Navy policy made a voucher approval checklist mandatory after audit findings that vouchers were being approved with missing or invalid receipts, missing or incomplete DD Form 1351-2s, and unsupported amounts. HQ RIO’s current “Big Six” guidance similarly emphasizes a small set of common errors that account for most returned submissions, reinforcing that AO workload is concentrated in a few recurring review categories. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

For an AO review assistant, the relevant design implication is not to replace the approver but to reduce triage burden. The evidence base supports an [[AI-assisted spec with human review]] model in which software surfaces likely problem areas, missing evidence, and policy-risk indicators while the AO remains the certifying human.
