# Judge-fit risk and claim calibration

Judge-fit risk is the chance that a project’s story, scope, or evidence set will not align with what judges are likely to value, even if the underlying prototype is technically solid. In the source material, this shows up as a need to keep “judge-fit language” softer, avoid turning public bios into certainty about preferences, and treat judge expectations as hypotheses rather than facts. The guidance is to tune the narrative using available public signals, but not to make unsupported claims about what judges “will” reward. ^[2026-04-25-codex-attack-on-genai-mil-research.md]

Claim calibration is the practice of matching every statement to the strength of its evidence. The source material recommends conservative phrasing for platform and adoption claims, avoiding overreach about offline operation, full user coverage, current rejection rates, direct system integration, or no-auth API access unless the evidence actually supports those points. It also distinguishes between a credible pain pattern and a current DoD-wide KPI, and warns against using single secondary sources as slide headlines without primary confirmation. ^[2026-04-25-codex-attack-on-genai-mil-research.md]

A practical calibration pattern is to frame the demo around what is directly demonstrable: a bounded workflow, visible before/after, cited rationale, missing-field detection, refusal when ungrounded, and a human review boundary. The material explicitly recommends claiming that the prototype produces a review-ready draft and supporting artifacts, while avoiding overstatements such as official submission, authoritative policy paraphrase without citation, or direct compatibility with downstream systems. Related concepts include [[AI-assisted spec with human review]], [[Controlled Reference Retrieval]], [[Citations and missing-field visibility]], and [[Scoped Prototype Wedge]]. ^[2026-04-25-codex-attack-on-genai-mil-research.md]

In judge-facing storytelling, the safest approach is to keep the wedge narrow and honest: explain the workflow, show the evidence, and describe the mechanism without inflating the operational claim. The source material suggests using public bios and organizer cues only to shape the presentation, not to assert likely scoring behavior. This is especially important when the project sits in a space where public evidence is mixed-quality or locally scoped, and where a technically interesting build can still fail if its claims exceed its proof. ^[2026-04-25-codex-attack-on-genai-mil-research.md]
