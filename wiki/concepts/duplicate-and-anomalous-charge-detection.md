# Duplicate and anomalous charge detection

Duplicate and anomalous charge detection is the practice of spotting repeated, inconsistent, or suspicious charges in a DTS voucher or related travel packet before approval. In the source material, this includes catching duplicate expenses, multiple charges where one would normally be expected, strange numbers that do not match expected patterns, and transactions that are hard to reconcile with receipts, dates, or the traveler’s stated purpose. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md, 2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The detection problem is part of a broader audit-like review workflow. A reviewer has to reconstruct a coherent financial story from messy transactions, incomplete receipts, memory gaps, and funding constraints, then decide whether the packet explains itself well enough for an accountable human reviewer. Duplicate or anomalous charges are therefore not just a bookkeeping issue; they are a signal that the packet may contain missing evidence, poor paperwork, or unclear authorization. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md]

Common patterns include duplicate expenses entered in more than one place, hotel costs that diverge from what the reviewer expects, unexplained deltas between claimed amounts and receipts, and cases involving cash, ATM withdrawals, exchange rates, or hand-calculated currency conversions. The source material also notes that missing dates, overlapping details, and odd amounts can make a packet look risky before a full review begins. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md]

In the official DTS review context, service guidance and public DTMO materials repeatedly identify duplicate expenses as a recurring return reason. AOs are expected to use standardized checklists to verify trip dates and locations, receipts, pre-audit flags, document validity, and related supporting records before approval. The checklist is meant to help surface problems like duplicate charges alongside missing receipts and mismatched documentation. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

For an AI-assisted reviewer, this kind of detection should be treated as a support function rather than a decision function. The tool can flag likely duplicates, highlight anomalous patterns, reconcile amounts against receipts or local paperwork, and draft concise human-review notes, while leaving approval, denial, and entitlement decisions to the human AO. Related concepts include [[AO checklist-driven voucher review]], [[AO checklist mandate and recurring service-level return reasons]], and [[AI-assisted spec with human review]]. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md, 2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md, 2026-04-24-hackathon-operating-context.md]

## Detection cues

- Duplicate expenses entered more than once
- Multiple charges where one is normally expected
- Amounts that do not match receipts or local paperwork
- Strange numbers or unexplained deltas
- Missing dates or overlapping details
- Cash, ATM, or exchange-rate cases that need extra reconciliation ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md]

## Why it matters

Duplicate and anomalous charges are a recurring source of voucher returns. Catching them early can reduce rework, improve documentation quality, and make it easier for an AO to confirm that the packet is valid, supported, and consistent with mission and policy requirements. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]
