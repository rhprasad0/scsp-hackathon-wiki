# Human approval boundary and trust limits for military travel AI

Human travel-voucher AI for DoD travel workflows should be designed as a triage and evidence-surfacing aid, not as an approver or certifier. In the DTS approval path, the Authorizing Official (AO) remains the human decision-maker with pecuniary liability, while existing controls such as Pre-Audit flags, Reason Codes, advisories, and the Travel Policy Compliance Tool already handle parts of the review process. A useful AI system therefore needs a clear boundary: it can help organize, retrieve, and explain, but it must not approve, deny, submit, certify, sign, or accuse fraud. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The trust limit is especially important because the AO’s role is legally and operationally narrow but consequential. Public guidance says AOs must verify mission necessity, compliance with the Joint Travel Regulations, supporting records, receipts, pre-audit flags, and reason codes; they must return documents with comments when compliance is not met. The same source material describes a repeated failure pattern in which missing or invalid receipts drive more than 70% of improper payments, showing why AI assistance should surface evidence gaps rather than try to override human judgment. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

A military travel AI should preserve a visible human-review step whenever the output affects payment, entitlement, or compliance. The source material repeatedly emphasizes that the AO is the human approver and that pecuniary liability attaches to the human certifying officer, not to software. Related concepts include [[AI-assisted spec with human review]], [[Controlled Reference Retrieval]], [[Citations and missing-field visibility]], and [[Human-in-the-Loop Review and Approval]]. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The safest design posture is to treat AI as a quality-assurance layer: it can rank voucher risk, check for missing or invalid receipts, compare claimed amounts against attached documentation, flag blank or vague justifications, and point the AO to the relevant policy references. It should not determine entitlement, make fraud accusations, or act as a substitute for the AO’s authorized review path. This keeps the system aligned with the public DoD trust boundary described in the source material and with the operational reality that current DTS and related controls are still centered on human approval. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]
