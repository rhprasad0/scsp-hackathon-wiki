# DoD AI Ethical Principles

The Department of Defense adopted five AI ethical principles: Responsible, Equitable, Traceable, Reliable, and Governable. These principles apply to both combat and non-combat functions, and the source material frames them as part of lawful behavior, trust, testing, fielding, and scaling. ^[2026-04-30-dod-ai-policy-bonfire.md]

**Responsible** means DoD personnel exercise appropriate judgment and care while remaining responsible for AI development, deployment, and use. The source material treats accountable human responsibility as central to this principle. Related concept: [[human-in-the-loop]]. ^[2026-04-30-dod-ai-policy-bonfire.md]

**Equitable** is referenced in the source material as one of the five principles, but the material does not provide a fuller definition beyond its inclusion in the DoD framework. The related policy framing emphasizes trust and responsible use across DoD AI capabilities. ^[2026-04-30-dod-ai-policy-bonfire.md]

**Traceable** means relevant personnel should understand the technology, development processes, operational methods, data sources, design procedures, and documentation. In the source material, traceability is tied to whether people can actually understand how the system works, not just whether records exist. Related concept: [[audit logs]]. ^[2026-04-30-dod-ai-policy-bonfire.md]

**Reliable** means AI systems need explicit, well-defined uses and testing or assurance across the lifecycle. The source material also links reliability to lifecycle evaluation, verification, and validation. ^[2026-04-30-dod-ai-policy-bonfire.md]

**Governable** means AI capabilities should detect and avoid unintended consequences and allow disengagement or deactivation when unintended behavior appears. The source material highlights the importance of a real stop path before finalization. Related concept: [[closed-loop adjudication pipeline]]. ^[2026-04-30-dod-ai-policy-bonfire.md]

The source material also presents these principles as a useful lens for evaluating whether a system is merely producing the artifacts of compliance or actually preserving human authority, bounded use, traceability, and governability. In that framing, a system can generate logs, rationales, and self-assessments while still failing the underlying ethical control assumptions. Related concepts include [[audit trail limits]], [[automation bias]], and [[decorative human review]]. ^[2026-04-30-dod-ai-policy-bonfire.md]
