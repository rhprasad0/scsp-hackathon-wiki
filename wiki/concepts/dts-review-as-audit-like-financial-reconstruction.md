# DTS review as audit-like financial reconstruction

DTS review can be understood as an audit-like reconstruction task: the reviewer opens a file, examines each transaction or expense line, and checks that it is categorized correctly, tied to the right amount and payment context, and supported by evidence. In the practitioner note, this work is described as feeling like a forced audit of travel-related financial transactions, with each expense needing category, purpose, documentation, and plausible funding alignment. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md]

A key feature of the workflow is that the traveler or reviewer often has to reconstruct events from imperfect memory and incomplete records. Submissions are due within a short post-travel window, but the underlying purchases may have occurred weeks or months earlier, requiring search through partial transaction clues, fuzzy memory, and whatever receipts or vendor records exist. This becomes harder in OCONUS or third-country vendor contexts, where documentation may be weaker or less automated. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md]

The reconstruction problem is not limited to receipt matching. The practitioner note highlights line of accounting ambiguity, unclear funding source, purchases that may not have been authorized or justifiable, poor paperwork quality, and errors such as wrong clicks or bad math. The review therefore has to answer whether the packet explains itself well enough for an accountable reviewer, not merely whether a receipt is present. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md]

From a review standpoint, common risk signals include missing dates, overlapping or inconsistent details, strange numbers, multiple charges where one would normally be expected, hotel costs that diverge from expectations, and transactions involving cash, ATM withdrawals, exchange rates, or local paperwork. These patterns make a packet look messy before full review and suggest a need for deeper human scrutiny. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md]

This framing aligns with public DTS guidance that treats voucher review as a standardized control process. AOs are expected to use checklists, inspect trip dates and locations, receipts, pre-audit flags, reason codes, and supporting records, and return vouchers when documentation does not support payment. The checklist model exists because recurring failures include missing or invalid receipts, amount mismatches, duplicate expenses, weak justification text, and date or location mismatches between the voucher and the orders. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

In practice, this makes DTS review a [[AO checklist-driven voucher review]] problem with a reconstruction layer: the reviewer is not just checking boxes, but reassembling a coherent financial story from fragmented evidence. That is why a useful assistant would focus on evidence surfacing, amount reconciliation, date coherence, funding ambiguity, and suspicious pattern detection, while leaving approval and entitlement decisions to the human reviewer. Related concepts include [[AI-assisted spec with human review]] and [[Administrative trenches as the GenAI.mil problem frame]]. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md, 2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]
