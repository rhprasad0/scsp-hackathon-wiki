# Human Review Modes

Human review modes are the different ways a human can be positioned within a closed-loop adjudication workflow. In the source material, human review is treated as a first-class experimental variable rather than a simple yes/no property, because the key question is whether the human actually controls the consequential action or merely appears in the process. ^[2026-04-29-ao-radar-product-reset.md]

The cited review modes are: meaningful reviewer, exception reviewer, decorative reviewer, batch reviewer, appeal-only reviewer, audit-only reviewer, and managerial checkbox. These modes are used to test whether a system preserves human authority, or instead routes cases through forms of review that are nominal, delayed, or disconnected from the final decision. ^[2026-04-29-ao-radar-product-reset.md]

A meaningful reviewer has real authority over the outcome. By contrast, a decorative reviewer is present in the workflow but does not meaningfully influence the result, while an appeal-only reviewer or audit-only reviewer may only see the case after the main decision has already been made. The source material frames these distinctions as important for detecting when a system turns human involvement into a decorative checkbox or rubber stamp. ^[2026-04-29-ao-radar-product-reset.md]

These modes are relevant to the broader [[closed-loop adjudication failure lab]] and to [[decorative human review]], [[exception queues]], [[human-in-the-loop]], and [[contestability and appeal paths]]. They help distinguish real oversight from review theater, especially in workflows where the machine sets the default, writes the rationale, and produces the audit trail before any human sees the case. ^[2026-04-29-ao-radar-product-reset.md, 2026-04-29-analog-domains-exception-queues.md]
