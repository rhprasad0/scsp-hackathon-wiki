# Predecessor military GenAI systems

Predecessor military GenAI systems are the earlier Department of Defense, Department of the Air Force, and Army generative AI efforts that preceded the current GenAI.mil platform. The source material describes them as the practical lineage behind today’s enterprise AI environment, with Task Force Lima as the early mapping effort, [[AI Rapid Capabilities Cell (AI RCC)]] as the successor operating body, and service-specific systems such as NIPRGPT and CamoGPT demonstrating demand, authorization patterns, and trust-boundary requirements. ^[2026-04-25-genai-mil-public-context-and-links.md]

## Task Force Lima

Task Force Lima was a CDAO standing task force chartered in August 2023 by Deputy Secretary of Defense Kathleen Hicks to assess generative AI and large language models for DoD use. It ran through December 11, 2024 and catalogued roughly 180–230+ candidate use cases, grouped into 15 areas spanning two broad categories: warfighting and enterprise management. ^[2026-04-25-genai-mil-public-context-and-links.md]

Task Force Lima matters as a predecessor because it established the first broad use-case map that later informed the enterprise AI transition. The source material treats its output as a foundation that should not be reinvented when building new military GenAI applications. ^[2026-04-25-genai-mil-public-context-and-links.md]

## [[AI Rapid Capabilities Cell (AI RCC)]]

The [[AI Rapid Capabilities Cell (AI RCC)]] succeeded Task Force Lima in December 2024 and is described as a Department of War/Defense generative AI operating body associated with the consolidation of AI efforts into an enterprise platform era. The source material says it was run jointly by the CDAO and the Defense Innovation Unit, later operating under the DoW Office of the Under Secretary for Research and Engineering, with linkage to the Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Office. ^[2026-04-25-genai-mil-public-context-and-links.md]

AI RCC took over Task Force Lima’s 15-use-case map and oriented it toward enterprise-management priorities rather than tactical use cases. Named priority areas include financial systems, human resources, enterprise logistics and supply chain, healthcare information management, legal analysis and compliance, procurement processes, and software development/cybersecurity. The source material also notes substantial early investment, including $100M in FY24/25 plus $35M for four “Frontier AI” 90-day pilots. ^[2026-04-25-genai-mil-public-context-and-links.md]

As a predecessor system, AI RCC is important because it frames the practical military AI problem space around administrative burden, back-office workflows, and operationally legible enterprise tasks rather than combat applications. ^[2026-04-25-genai-mil-public-context-and-links.md]

## NIPRGPT

NIPRGPT was a Department of the Air Force experimental ChatGPT-equivalent launched in June 2024 on NIPRNet. The source material describes it as IL4 and CAC-gated, built on the Dark Saber platform, and capable of reaching about 80,000 users in three months. ^[2026-04-25-genai-mil-public-context-and-links.md]

NIPRGPT is a useful predecessor example because it shows an early authorized Air Force deployment and a moving approval environment. The source material notes that a later Military Review article reported it had “lost its approval,” which should be read as evidence that authorization status can change over time rather than as a fixed architectural judgment. ^[2026-04-25-genai-mil-public-context-and-links.md]

## CamoGPT

CamoGPT was an Army GenAI tool in 2024 with NIPR and SIPR deployments. The source material says it supported RAG, file uploads, persistent chat, and preset prompts, with NIPR at IL5 including CUI and SIPR at IL6. ^[2026-04-25-genai-mil-public-context-and-links.md]

CamoGPT is significant because it illustrates how military GenAI systems began to incorporate retrieval grounding, trusted document workflows, and classification-bound deployment patterns. The source material advises using it to motivate trust boundaries rather than to infer internal consolidation details. ^[2026-04-25-genai-mil-public-context-and-links.md]

## CENTGPT, DHSChat, Sage AI, and Spark Cell tools

The source material also mentions CENTGPT, DHSChat, Sage AI, and Spark Cell tools as additional service or agency variants noted in DoD tech press. These systems are presented as part of the broader predecessor landscape that validated demand and surfaced requirements such as multi-cloud portability, model agnosticism, and baked-in safety guardrails. ^[2026-04-25-genai-mil-public-context-and-links.md]

## Why these predecessors matter

Taken together, these systems form the lineage that led into the current enterprise GenAI.mil environment. The progression runs from use-case discovery and mapping, to enterprise-aligned consolidation, to early service-specific deployments that demonstrated the need for retrieval grounding, trust boundaries, authorization controls, and reviewable outputs.
