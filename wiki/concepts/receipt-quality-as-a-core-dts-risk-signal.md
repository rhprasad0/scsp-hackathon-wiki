# Receipt quality as a core DTS risk signal

Receipt quality is a central signal for DTS voucher risk because reviewers repeatedly rely on receipts to confirm that claimed expenses are valid, supported, and match the underlying transaction. Public guidance and practitioner observations both point to missing, invalid, inadequate, or otherwise weak receipts as a major driver of voucher returns, amount mismatches, and higher review burden. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md, 2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md]

In the practitioner notes, the review process is described as an audit-like reconstruction of travel spending: each expense must have an amount, category, funding logic, and supporting evidence. The note specifically highlights missing receipts, unclear or weak vendor documentation, and poor paper trails as common failure reasons, especially when travelers are reconstructing purchases from memory after a delay. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md]

Public DTS guidance reinforces the same point. AO checklist materials emphasize verifying receipts, and DTMO reports that more than 70% of improper payments are due to inadequate or invalid receipts. Service guidance also treats receipt problems as recurring return triggers, including missing lodging, airline, and rental car documentation. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

Receipt quality is not only about whether a receipt exists. The practitioner response shows that reviewers also care about whether the receipt is legible, tied to the right transaction, and reconcilable with card charges, exchange-rate math, or local paperwork. A packet can look risky when amounts do not match, when receipts are hard to interpret, or when reconstruction depends on fuzzy memory and manual calculation. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md]

This makes receipt quality a practical [[risk signal]] for [[DTS]] triage tools and [[AO checklist-driven voucher review]] workflows. A useful assistant should not decide entitlement or approve payment; instead, it should flag weak or missing receipts, surface amount discrepancies, identify transactions that need human scrutiny, and help draft concise return comments for the reviewer. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md, 2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

Receipt quality also intersects with harder edge cases. The practitioner notes point out that OCONUS, third-country, cash, ATM, and exchange-rate situations can produce weaker documentation without implying traveler negligence. In those cases, the signal is not simply “no receipt,” but whether the available evidence still tells a coherent, reviewable financial story. ^[2026-04-25-dts-practitioner-response-batch-1.md]
