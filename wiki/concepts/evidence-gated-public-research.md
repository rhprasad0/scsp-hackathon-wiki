# Evidence-gated public research

Evidence-gated public research is a workflow rule for when to use outside or public research: it should not be automatic, and it should be used only when a specific evidence gap is worth filling. In the operating context, the existing evidence base is treated as early hypothesis scaffolding rather than validated SME signal, so public research serves as a targeted way to sharpen a question rather than a default source of truth. ^[2026-04-24-hackathon-operating-context.md]

The pattern fits a private working-memory process in which raw observations are captured privately, promoted into redacted curated notes, ingested into a compiled wiki, and then queried for repeated pain points, workflows, trust boundaries, and candidate wedges. Public research comes later, after synthesis, when the team needs to resolve a concrete gap in the current understanding rather than expand scope broadly. ^[2026-04-24-hackathon-operating-context.md]

This approach is meant to keep hackathon discovery interview-efficient and opportunistic. Because access to DoD-experienced participants may be uneven and the event timeline is short, the workflow favors narrow reusable specs over broad prototype sprawl, with outside research reserved for cases where it clearly improves the evidence base. Related concepts include [[AI-assisted spec with human review]], [[Controlled Reference Retrieval]], [[Current-State Workflow Interviewing]], and [[Scoped Prototype Wedge]]. ^[2026-04-24-hackathon-operating-context.md]

In practice, evidence-gated public research supports public-safe outputs by keeping raw notes private by default, sanitizing any downstream export, and preserving human review. It also helps prevent the AI system from inventing operational facts, since research is used selectively to answer a defined gap rather than to fill in missing context indiscriminately. ^[2026-04-24-hackathon-operating-context.md]
