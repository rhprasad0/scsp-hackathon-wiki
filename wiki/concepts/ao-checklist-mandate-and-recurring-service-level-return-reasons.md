# AO checklist mandate and recurring service-level return reasons

The public record shows that DTS approving officials (AOs) are expected to use a standardized checklist when reviewing vouchers, and that several services have turned that expectation into an explicit mandate. The strongest public example is the Navy’s voucher approval checklist requirement, which was made mandatory in 2019 and was tied to recurring audit findings about incorrect approvals, missing receipts, and incomplete voucher documentation. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

DTMO guidance likewise instructs AOs to use a standardized process or checklist to reduce mistakes and oversights, and to return documents when expenses are not within mission or when a request for payment is inappropriate. The AO checklist materials emphasize checking trip dates and locations, travel mode, receipts, pre-audit flags, reason codes, advisories, and document validity before approval. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

At the service level, the same return reasons recur across official guidance and policy notices. The most common patterns are missing or invalid receipts, amount mismatches between claimed expenses and attached documentation, duplicate expenses, weak or absent justification text, missing signature or DD Form 1351-2 issues, and date/location mismatches between the voucher and underlying orders. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The Navy checklist is especially explicit about what AOs must verify. It requires review across multiple DTS screens and includes checks for duplicate expenses, lodging and airline receipts, rental car final receipts and charges, proper reason codes for approval-after-trip-start cases, and the presence of meaningful justification rather than placeholder text. Related concepts include [[Army regulation Q&A with paragraph-level citations]] and [[Back-office military workflow examples]]. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

The recurrence of these return reasons is not just anecdotal. DTMO reports that more than 70% of improper payments are due to inadequate or invalid receipts, and DoD oversight reports continue to identify travel pay as a persistent payment-integrity problem. That combination helps explain why service-level checklist mandates have become a control response rather than just a local preference. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

## Common recurring return reasons

- Missing, invalid, or inadequate receipts, especially for lodging, rental car, and airfare
- Claimed amounts that do not match attached receipts
- Duplicate expenses entered in more than one place
- Blank, one-character, or otherwise non-informative justification text
- Missing or incomplete DD Form 1351-2 / wet signature path for NDEA-entered vouchers
- Date or location mismatches between the voucher and the travel orders
- Incorrect per diem, lodging-tax, or meal handling based on the trip type
- Missing oral-order details when approval occurs after trip start ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

## Service-level examples

### Navy

The Navy’s voucher approval checklist was made mandatory in June 2019. Publicly posted checklist language shows a broad, screen-by-screen review that includes trip details, expenses, per diem, accounting, financial summary, pre-audit items, and final submission. The same policy set was introduced in response to audit findings that approved DTS vouchers did not match receipts, and it explicitly calls out missing or invalid lodging, rental car, and airline documentation as recurring problems. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

### Air Force Reserve / HQ RIO

HQ RIO’s “Big Six” policy is a current unit-level example of recurring return reasons becoming a formalized checklist. It identifies six common errors that account for most returned submissions: missing or incorrect orders or line of accounting, date/location mismatches, annual training meal and per diem issues, lodging discrepancies, missing documentation, and rental car proration problems. The policy is explicitly framed as a response to reduced AO staffing and to backlog risk. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

### Marine Corps and other local policies

Marine Corps and local policy notices reinforce the same pattern by warning AOs that vague approval text such as “auth,” “approved,” or a period is insufficient, and that pre-audit flags need written explanations. This shows that recurring return reasons are not limited to missing receipts; they also include weak justification discipline and incomplete rationale for flagged items. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

## Why the checklist matters

The checklist functions as a control surface for a role that carries pecuniary liability and is often performed as an additional duty. In practice, the checklist is meant to standardize review, reduce avoidable errors, and make it easier to identify the small set of recurring failure modes that drive most returns. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]

For a [[Back-office military workflow examples|back-office workflow]] like DTS voucher review, this means the AO is not expected to memorize every rule from scratch. Instead, service and DTMO guidance converge on a repeated workflow: verify the supporting evidence, check policy fit, require meaningful justification, and return the voucher when the documentation does not support payment. ^[2026-04-25-dts-ao-radar-public-research.md]
